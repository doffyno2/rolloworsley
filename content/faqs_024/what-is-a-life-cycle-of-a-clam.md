Clams are bivalve molluscs that can be found in both fresh and saltwater environments, ranging in size from the small, nearly microscopic species, to the enormous geoduck clam. Clams possess a complex life cycle that consists of several distinct stages, each of which is necessary for the species to survive and reproduce. 

<h2>Eggs and Sperm Production</h2>

The first step in the life cycle of a clam involves the production of both eggs and sperm. This process is known as spawning, and it is triggered by environmental cues such as temperature and light. During spawning, clams release their eggs and sperm into the water, where the two meet and fertilization occurs. 

<h2>The Veliger Larvae</h2>

Once fertilization has occurred, the egg develops into a veliger larvae. The veliger larvae is a free-swimming organism that moves through the water and feeds on microscopic organisms. After a certain period of time, the veliger larvae will settle onto a suitable substrate and undergo metamorphosis into the next stage of its life cycle. 

<h2>The Juvenile Clam</h2>

The juvenile clam is the next stage in the life cycle of a clam and is the stage at which the clam takes on its adult form. During this stage, the clam’s shell is soft and its body is still in the process of growing and developing. The shell will slowly harden and become more protective as the clam continues to grow and mature. 

<h2>The Adult Clam</h2>

The adult clam is the final stage in the life cycle of a clam. At this stage, the clam’s shell is fully formed and its body has reached full maturity. The adult clam is now capable of reproducing and will begin the cycle again with the production of eggs and sperm.
